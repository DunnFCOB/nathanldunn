In December of 2025, Australian Jews were tragically targeted by gunmen at a public Hanukkah celebration in Bondi Beach. It was [the deadliest terrorist incident in Australia’s history](https://www.britannica.com/event/2025-Bondi-Beach-shooting), and resulted in fifteen dead. In response, less than a month later, the [Royal Commission on Antisemitism and Social Cohesion]([https://asc.royalcommission.gov.au](https://asc.royalcommission.gov.au/)) was established to formally investigate the state of antisemitism in the country and to provide recommendations on how the government can better protect Australia’s Jewish community. 

Then, in April of this year, a benefit concert that was planned to take place at the Sydney Town Hall to benefit the Bondi Beach attack victims and their families was cancelled. The news came as a shock after it was [suddenly announced](https://www.timesofisrael.com/bondi-benefit-concert-canceled-after-choir-opposes-singing-with-jewish-group/amp/) that more than half of the local Australian Hellenic Choir, who were slated to sing alongside the Sydney Jewish Choral Society, “politically objected” to performing with the Jewish choir and voted not to take part in the concert. 

The implication here being that Jewish identity and peoplehood is, on its own, a political act. The Australian Hellenic Choir president, James Tsolakis, argued he understood that what his choir had done was just that:

> Unfortunately, we have a lot of people in the community blaming the Jewish community for what’s happening in Israel, Palestine… that’s not correct.

Though, the objecting members of the choir claimed the decision is merely political in nature, and as most western democracies would rightly argue, political speech should be protected. While the possibility remains that these members of the choir could genuinely believe they’re merely standing up for their political beliefs, hate isn't based on intent. It’s felt in its effect. 

And this was a Jewish choir, performing at a concert to benefit victims of an anti-Jewish terrorist attack. How then is performing at such a concert alongside Jews a political act? And if simply performing alongside Jews is political in nature, then in what manner are Jews meant to exist as not to breach this societal contract on acceptability? 

The answer here is that, by politically objecting solely on the basis of Jewish identity, these members of the Australian Hellenic Choir determined that Jews are collectively guilty for the actions of a foreign government. That isn’t criticism of Israel, it’s anti-Jewish rhetoric which they attempted to repackage as a personal political belief. 

When Elias Rodriguez, after [murdering Sarah Milgrim and Yaron Lischinsky](https://www.justice.gov/usao-dc/pr/new-terrorism-related-charges-filed-against-alleged-killer-israeli-embassy-employees)—execution style—outside of the Capital Jewish Museum in Washington DC at an event for communal bridge building, shouted “Free Palestine” at a group of shocked bystanders, it wasn’t a critique of Israel. When Mohamed Sabry Soliman deployed a makeshift flamethrower and threw Molotov cocktails into a crowd of people marching in Boulder, CO for the release of hostages held in Gaza, [murdering 82-year-old Karen Diamond]([https://bouldercolorado.gov/news/city-statement-may-7-2026-boulder-attack-plea-hearing](https://bouldercounty.gov/news/remembrance-resilience-and-community/)), it wasn’t driven by political ideology. When a Hezbollah-inspired attacker [drove a truck full of fuel and explosives into Temple Israel of West Bloomfield, MI](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/09/nx-s1-5810635/the-aftermath-is-just-devastating-temple-israel-rebuilds-after-terror-attack) while over 100 Jewish children were still inside, and admitted prior to the attack in videos sent to his sister that he wanted to kill as many Jews as he could, it can’t be argued that it was just a political disagreement. 

These proposed justifications attempt to legitimize hateful rhetoric by convincing others that no, they don’t actually hate Jews, they just hate Israel. But when—as a means of “politically objecting” to Israeli government policy—they de-platform *Jewish* creators, antagonize *Jewish* students, attack *Jewish* synagogues, and murder *Jewish* people, that attempted justification falls flat. 

I've sat for Friday night Shabbat services and listened to Jewish teens share their lived experiences of antisemitism directed at them by their own classmates. I've heard from non-Jewish colleagues, who told of how they were verbally threatened for simply wearing a pin in support of the Jewish community after October 7th, 2023. Ask almost anyone, Jewish or not, who regularly engages with Jewish communal life, and they'll tell you about a recent experience they had or witnessed which they felt was based in antisemitism. The statistics—based on data gathered from organizations ranging from anti-discrimination non-profits, polling groups, official government institutions, and everything in between—bear out what the lived experience already confirms.

State-sponsored discrimination doesn’t turn into action overnight. It starts with words, and the words from a recent Democratic political candidate from Texas, Maureen Galindo, were that, were she elected, she would repurpose a local ICE detention facility to a "prison for American Zionists and former ICE officers for human trafficking." She then went on to say, "It will also be a castration processing center for pedophiles which will probably be most of the Zionists." [All posted to her personal Instagram account.](https://www.instagram.com/p/DYRyMXOFQCN/?img_index=6&igsh=MTlicmZoaWYzdjkzZA==)

Thankfully, Galindo's ambitions failed to come to fruition, and most prominent Democrats, including well-known politicians like Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, [rightfully condemned her comments as antisemitic](https://x.com/aoc/status/2056879559463547218?s=46). Even so, that didn't stop Galindo from still [receiving 36% of the vote in her district](https://electionresults.sos.state.tx.us/results.html). Imagine that happening ten years ago, just as Trump was gearing up to win his first presidential election, then telling the Jewish community that "antisemitism isn't actually that bad.”

The difference between then and now is that antisemitic rhetoric has been reframed as legitimate political discourse. What was once considered an antisemitic trope is now legitimized as "political discussion," even amongst some Jewish communities, and while Galindo is an extreme case, the normalization of antisemitic rhetoric doesn’t require extremists. 

The accusation that Israel commits “genocide” in Gaza being one such example. Genocide is a fixed legal definition under the 1948 Convention requiring proven specific intent to destroy a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group. Yet even prominent genocide scholars like Israeli-American Omer Bartov, the Dean's Professor of Holocaust and Genocide Studies at Brown University, have accused Israel of "incremental genocide"—a non-binding scholarly redefinition that adds elements the 1948 Convention doesn’t contain. Bartov himself acknowledges there's “no consensus” on this charge, yet according to a [recent analysis](https://isgap.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/OP-08-Soffer.pdf) of his claims by the Institute for the Study of Global Antisemitism and Policy (ISGAP)—an organization which focuses specifically on documenting antisemitism in academic contexts—Bartov stretches the term for political and activist purposes, leaning heavily on emotion over evidentiary rigor.

This expansionist approach becomes antisemitic through its singular application. These same scholars who level genocide charges at Israel—the only Jewish nation in the world—don't concern themselves equally with Russia's campaign in Ukraine, China's documented destruction of the Uyghur people, or atrocities in Syria, Yemen, and Myanmar. Where accusations at other countries invoke the Convention's standard terms, accusations at Israel rely on redefined, non-binding scholarly frameworks that the accusers themselves acknowledge lack consensus.

The “incremental genocide” framework also constitutes Holocaust inversion, a form of antisemitism that transforms Jews from Holocaust victims into Holocaust perpetrators. A Jewish Holocaust scholar accusing Jews of genocide amplifies this inversion’s credibility while perverting the term’s original purpose: Raphael Lemkin coined “genocide” specifically to describe crimes akin to the Nazi regime's systematic destruction of over 6 million Jews, the most extreme case of mass extermination, and a massacre which to this day—over 80 years later—the global Jewish population has yet to recover from. 

When antisemitic rhetoric is reframed as “political discussion” in this way, it gains social acceptability. Take the recent speech given by NYC mayor Zohran Mamdani as an example. In his speech, he said the following to applause:

> The monsters that we are up against, they take many different forms today... In AIPAC, for whom the only thing more frightening than democracy being allowed to run its course is an end to genocide and Netanyahu’s wars. They move millions in dark money to accomplish a single goal, to preserve their power so that they can turn us against one another instead of our leaders turning towards the moral change we all know to be necessary.

This rhetoric relies heavily on classical antisemitic tropes that have been used to paint Jews as “other” and “inferior” for centuries. For a deeper breakdown on this specific instance, see my recent essay [here](https://open.substack.com/pub/nathanldunn/p/remastering-the-classics). 

Since that essay, over 800 rabbis across the US, including my family’s rabbi who provided services for both my father and grandfather at their respective funerals, have publicly signed [an open letter](https://jewishmajority.org/open-letter-from-rabbis) demanding that Mamdani apologize for his “dangerous, unacceptable” remarks. At the time of writing, no such apology has occurred. Instead, Mamdani doubled-down on his previous remarks despite the explicit condemnations from Jewish communal and organizational leaders. 

When this type of antisemitism gains social acceptance, not only by ordinary citizens but by those in positions of power, it becomes exceedingly dangerous. 

The US has historically been an exception in a world that for thousands of years has discriminated against Jews, and this is by design. With how US law is currently designed—even if public sentiment massively shifted against not just Israel but Jews as a whole—a politician attempting to strip Jews of our rights and protections afforded to us would immediately be met with massive roadblocks set forth by an extensive legal architecture meant to prevent exactly such a scenario. 

This architecture isn’t always perfect though, and it won’t entirely prevent abuses from happening. There will always be loopholes, and antisemitism will always contort and mutate to meet the needs of the antisemite. 

When societies praised the communist ideal, Jews were accused of being agents of Western capitalism. In societies which sought capitalistic economies, Jews were accused of “Judeo-Bolshevism” and of attempting to install communist policies via subterfuge. When societies valued racial purity and white-supremacism, Jews were considered non-white and inferior. Today, as most progressive western societies now see “whiteness” and religiosity as oppressive, Jews are now accused of "white, settler-colonialism" and of building a theocratic ethnostate which oppresses Palestinians. These accusations all came about as mere “political discussion,” which has always served as the most dangerous mechanism by which antisemitic rhetoric spreads, and this ancient hatred will continue to do so as long as society allows it to hide in plain sight.
