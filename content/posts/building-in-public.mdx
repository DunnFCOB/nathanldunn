Sharing work before it's ready is uncomfortable. It's also, I've come to think, the only honest way to build anything meant to be used by other people.

I spent most of my career doing the opposite — keeping things close until they felt finished, then releasing them into a silence that was hard to interpret. Were people indifferent? Confused? Had I solved the wrong problem entirely?

Building in public forces the question earlier, when there's still time to do something about the answer.

## What "in public" actually means

It doesn't mean broadcasting every half-formed thought. It means making the process legible — showing what you're working on, what's blocking you, and what you're uncertain about, in a way that invites real response.

The goal isn't visibility. It's feedback with enough context to be useful.

## The discomfort is the point

There's a filtering effect that happens when you know someone might read what you're writing before it's polished. You're forced to articulate things you'd otherwise leave vague. Vagueness, it turns out, is often where the bad assumptions live.

I don't think this works for everything. Some work needs to be protected while it's fragile. But for things meant to eventually reach people — writing, software, policy proposals — the discomfort of early exposure is usually worth it.
