There's a tendency to watch national politics as if it were the only game in town. Cable news, social media, and the sheer spectacle of federal dysfunction all conspire to make Washington feel like the only arena that matters.

But spend time in any American city and a different picture emerges. The decisions that shape daily life — whether your street floods, whether your kids can walk to school, whether your neighborhood gets a grocery store or a parking lot — are made by people whose names don't appear on most ballots.

## The gap between attention and impact

Local governments control land use, building codes, water systems, transit, and the bulk of public education. These aren't secondary concerns. They're the infrastructure of ordinary life.

> "All politics is local" has become a cliché precisely because it's true in a way that's easy to forget.

The challenge is that local governance is genuinely hard to follow. Meeting agendas are buried in municipal websites. Decisions happen in rooms most people never enter. The press corps has thinned to almost nothing in most cities.

## What changes when you pay attention

I've spent the last several years watching Detroit's planning commission more closely than I'd like to admit. What I've found is that the gap between the decisions being made and public awareness of them is enormous — and that gap is where a lot of harm quietly accumulates.

It's also where change is most tractable. A determined group of ten people showing up to a zoning board meeting can shift outcomes in ways that a Twitter campaign with ten thousand participants cannot.

This is less inspiring than it sounds. It requires patience, tolerance for procedural tedium, and the willingness to lose repeatedly before anything moves. But it's real leverage, which is rarer than people think.
